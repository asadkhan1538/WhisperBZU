Definition

The abilities to interpret and reason with quantitative information—information that involves mathematical ideas or numbers. These abilities are crucial aspects of literacy.

Examples:

  • Comparing prices in the market to find the best deal
  • Interpreting health statistics, like blood pressure readings
  • Planning budgets and managing personal finance
  • Understanding percentages, like discounts, interest rates, or inflation

Literacy

Literacy is the ability to read and write. Some people can recognize only a few words and write only their names; others read and write in many languages. A primary goal of our educational system is to provide citizens with a level of literacy sufficient to read, write, and reason about the important issues of our time.

Logic

Logic is the study of the methods and principles of reasoning

Argument

An argument uses a set of facts or assumptions, called premises, to support a conclusion

Fallacy

A fallacy is a deceptive argument—an argument in which the conclusion is not well supported by the premises

Proposition

Propositions are statements that make (propose) a claim that may be either true or false. A proposition must have the structure of a complete sentence and must make a distinct assertion or denial.

Example: '7 + 9 = 2' is a proposition, even though false. 'Are you going?' is NOT a proposition.

Negation

The opposite of a proposition is called its negation. Negations are also propositions because they have complete sentence structure and may be true or false.

Example: Negation of 'Joan is sitting' is 'Joan is not sitting'

And Statements (Conjunctions)

An 'and' statement is called a conjunction. The conjunction p and q is true only if p and q are both true.

Example: 'Hard test AND got A' is true only if both are true

Or Statements (Disjunctions)

A compound statement made with 'or' is called a disjunction. We assume inclusive 'or' - true if either/both propositions are true.

Example: Inclusive: 'illness or injury' covers both. Exclusive: 'soup or salad' means one choice.

If...Then Statements

Conditional propositions propose something true (then part) on condition something else is true (if part).

Example: 'If politicians liars, then Smith liar'

Statistics

Methods for analyzing data and drawing conclusions from numerical information

Mean

The arithmetic average of a set of numbers - sum of all values divided by number of values

Example: For 2, 4, 6, 8: Mean = (2+4+6+8)/4 = 5

Median

The middle value in a set of numbers when arranged in numerical order

Example: For 1, 3, 5, 7, 9: Median = 5

Mode

The value that appears most frequently in a data set

Example: For 2, 3, 3, 5, 7: Mode = 3

Precision

Refers to the consistency and repeatability of measurements - indicates how close repeated measurements are to each other

Accuracy

Refers to how close a measurement is to the true or accepted value - indicates the correctness of a measurement

Ratio

A comparison of two quantities by division - shows relative size of two quantities

Example: Ratio of boys to girls: 3:2

Proportion

An equation that states that two ratios are equal - expresses equivalence of two ratios

Example: 2/4 = 1/2 is a proportion

Probability

The study of chance and uncertainty - measures likelihood that event will occur (0=impossible to 1=certain)

Example: Probability of heads in coin flip: 0.5

Equation

A mathematical statement showing equality of two expressions with equals sign

Example: 2x + 3 = 7

Average

A single value representing central tendency of a set of values - summarizes typical value in data set

Angle

Geometric figure formed by two rays sharing common endpoint (vertex) - measured in degrees/radians

Example: Right angle = 90 degrees

Quadrilateral

Polygon with four sides and four angles - sum of interior angles is 360 degrees

Example: Squares, rectangles, parallelograms, trapezoids
